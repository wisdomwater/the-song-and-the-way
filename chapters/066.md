## 66 — The Sacredness of Ordinary Duties

> *Freedom is not found by escaping responsibility.  
> It is found by learning how to carry responsibility without being possessed by it.*

## The Song

Krishna spoke again
about renunciation.

Many believed,
He said,
that holiness meant
walking away from life.

Leaving behind
work,
responsibility,
difficulty,
and obligation.

But Krishna challenged this.

The problem
is not action itself.

The problem
is attachment.

Some abandon responsibility
out of confusion.

Some out of fear.

Some because the work
is difficult,
painful,
or exhausting.

But this,
He said,
is not true freedom.

No one living
within a body
can completely stop acting.

Life itself
is movement.

Breathing.

Speaking.

Choosing.

Serving.

Creating.

Caring.

Even withdrawal
becomes its own kind
of action.

So the deeper question
is not:

“Will I act?”

But rather:

“How will I act?”

Will I cling
to outcomes?

Will I define myself
through success?

Will I resent difficult duties
and chase only pleasant ones?

Or will I learn
to serve faithfully
without being owned
by reward,
recognition,
or fear?

The wise person,
Krishna said,
does not hate difficult work.

Nor become attached
to comfortable work.

They act
because love
and truth
ask something of them.

And then
they let go.

### Along the Way

This teaching cuts deeply
against modern culture.

We often imagine freedom
as the absence
of responsibility.

“If I could just escape this job…”

“If I didn’t have obligations…”

“If nobody needed anything from me…”

“Then I would finally be free.”

But Krishna suggests
that inner bondage
travels with us.

A person may flee
their responsibilities
yet remain chained
to ego,
comfort,
fear,
or endless self-concern.

And another person
may quietly carry
ordinary burdens
while becoming inwardly free.

There is something sacred here
about ordinary life.

Raising children.

Showing up for work.

Caring for aging parents.

Paying bills honestly.

Keeping promises.

Doing difficult things
without resentment.

These are not obstacles
to spiritual life.

They can become
the very ground
where the soul matures.

Krishna is not glorifying
burnout
or self-erasure.

He is inviting us
into another relationship
with action itself.

To act sincerely.

To fulfill what is ours to do.

But to stop demanding
that every action
guarantee comfort,
status,
certainty,
or applause.

This changes everything.

Because when we release
our obsession
with outcomes,
even difficult responsibilities
can become lighter.

Not because the work disappears.

But because the ego
is no longer carrying
the crushing burden
of proving itself
through every action.

The heart begins
to discover
a quieter freedom:

“I will offer what I can.
And I do not need
to possess the result.”

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“Teach me faithful action”

Breathing out:
“Teach me freedom within responsibility”
