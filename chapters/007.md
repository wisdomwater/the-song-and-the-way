## 7 — What Cannot Be Destroyed

> *Beneath the changing forms of life there is something death cannot touch.  
> The soul trembles because it mistakes the temporary for the eternal.*

## The Song

Arjuna fell silent.

The warrior who had conquered enemies could no longer conquer the sorrow within himself.

“I will not fight,” he said.

And the words hung in the air between the two armies.

Then Krishna smiled.

Not mockingly.
Not dismissively.

But with the calm compassion of one watching a beloved friend awaken from a frightening dream.

“You speak as though you understand wisdom,” he said,
“yet you grieve for what the wise do not grieve.”

The battlefield remained unchanged.
The armies still waited.
The danger had not vanished.

Yet Krishna began speaking from another horizon entirely.

“There was never a time when you did not exist.
Nor I.
Nor any of these gathered here.

And there will never come a time when we cease to be.”

The words opened like a doorway beyond fear.

Bodies change.

Childhood fades into youth.
Youth softens into age.
One season of life gives way to another.

The body shifts endlessly,
like clothing worn for a moment and then laid aside.

But beneath all change,
something remains.

Something untouched.

The deepest self is not born when the body is born.
Nor destroyed when the body falls away.

It cannot be cut.
Cannot be burned.
Cannot be undone by time.

Krishna spoke of the passing sensations that dominate human life.

Pleasure and pain.
Heat and cold.
Gain and loss.

All arrive.
All depart.

Like weather crossing the sky.

The one who learns to endure these changing movements without being ruled by them begins to awaken into freedom.

Most people cling desperately to what cannot last.

Youth.
Possession.
Recognition.
Control.
Identity.

And so every change feels like death.

But the wise learn to rest in something deeper than passing conditions.

The eternal cannot be destroyed by the temporary.

And what is temporary was never meant to be clung to forever.

### Along the Way

Much of human suffering comes from confusion about what we truly are.

We build our identity around changing things:
our appearance,
our success,
our roles,
our relationships,
our emotions,
our fears.

And because these things constantly shift, the heart lives in continual anxiety.

Krishna’s teaching does not deny grief or pain. Instead, it invites a deeper perspective beneath them. The body changes. Circumstances change. Seasons of life rise and fall. Yet consciousness itself — the witnessing presence beneath experience — is not reduced to these temporary forms.

There is something within us that remains through every transformation.

The child.
The adult.
The aging body.
The grieving self.
The joyful self.

Awareness continues beneath them all.

To remember this changes how we move through suffering.

Pleasure no longer needs to be grasped desperately.
Pain no longer needs to define us permanently.

Both become passing experiences rather than absolute truths.

This is not emotional numbness.

It is steadiness.

The mature heart learns how to feel deeply without becoming imprisoned by every changing wave of experience.

Modern life trains people to chase comfort endlessly and flee discomfort at all costs. Yet a soul built entirely upon comfort becomes fragile. The smallest loss feels catastrophic. The smallest disruption creates fear.

Spiritual maturity grows through learning to remain grounded even as life changes around us.

Not detached from love.

Detached from illusion.

The invitation here is not to escape humanity,
but to remember that we are more than the temporary forms we inhabit.

And when we begin to live from that deeper place,
fear loosens its grip.

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“I am more than this moment”

Breathing out:
“Help me rest in what endures”
