## 38 — When the Veil Falls Away

> *The soul longs to see the Infinite.  
> Yet true vastness can undo us.*

## The Song

Arjuna spoke softly
to Krishna.

The confusion that once clouded me
has begun to lift.

Through Your mercy,
I now understand
that all things arise from You
and return again into You.

But hearing was no longer enough.

Arjuna wanted to see.

If it is possible,
he said,
show me Your eternal form.

Show me the fullness
of what You truly are.

And Krishna answered:

You cannot behold this
with ordinary eyes.

I will give you another kind of vision.

Then the veil opened.

The universe broke apart
into revelation.

Arjuna saw
countless forms
within Krishna.

Infinite faces.

Infinite eyes.

Infinite expressions
of majesty and terror and beauty.

The radiance was unbearable.

As though thousands of suns
had risen at once
and flooded existence
with blinding fire.

Within that form
Arjuna saw everything.

Gods.

Sages.

Worlds.

Creatures.

Stars.

The movement of creation itself.

All beings
contained within the body
of the Eternal.

No beginning.

No ending.

No center.

No edge.

Only vastness upon vastness.

The universe
alive inside the Divine.

Arjuna trembled.

Wonder overtook him.

His body shook.

His hair stood on end.

And falling forward in awe,
he bowed.

The One standing beside him
as friend,
guide,
and charioteer
was suddenly revealed
as immeasurable.

Beyond all categories.

Beyond comprehension.

The Infinite
had become visible.

And it was beautiful.

And terrifying.

### Along the Way

This passage touches something
both ancient
and deeply human:

the longing to encounter ultimate reality directly.

Many people hunger
for certainty.

For undeniable spiritual experience.

For revelation so overwhelming
that all doubt disappears forever.

But the Gita shows us
that encountering the Infinite
is not merely comforting.

It is destabilizing.

The ego wants transcendence
that remains manageable.

Beautiful.

Safe.

Contained within familiar categories.

But genuine encounters
with mystery
often undo our illusions.

They expose how small
our ordinary frameworks really are.

Throughout history,
people have described moments like this:

standing beneath immense silence,
overwhelmed by beauty,
struck by awe,
suddenly aware
of the fragility
and vastness of existence.

For a moment,
the self no longer feels central.

Something immeasurably larger
breaks through.

And strangely,
these moments can produce
both fear
and liberation.

Fear,
because control dissolves.

Liberation,
because the burden
of being the center of reality
begins to loosen.

The Gita also reminds us
that spiritual perception
requires more than physical sight.

Krishna tells Arjuna plainly:
ordinary eyes are not enough.

The deepest truths
cannot always be grasped
through analysis alone.

They require another kind of seeing.

A seeing shaped by humility,
surrender,
attention,
and grace.

And yet,
even here,
the story carries tenderness.

The Infinite does not remain distant.

The One who contains galaxies
still speaks personally
to Arjuna.

Still listens.

Still guides.

Still draws near.

The mystery is not cold.

It is relational.

And perhaps this is part
of the transformation unfolding here:

Arjuna no longer sees the Divine
merely as teacher or companion.

He begins to realize
that every form,
every life,
every movement of existence
rests within a Presence
far beyond what the mind
can fully contain.

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“Open my inner sight”

Breathing out:
“Teach me holy wonder”
