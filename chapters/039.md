## 39 — The Terror of the Infinite

> *The Holy is not only gentle light.  
> It is also the power before which every illusion trembles.*

## The Song

Arjuna kept looking.

The vision widened
beyond beauty.

Krishna’s form burned
in every direction.

Crowned with splendor.

Holding weapons of heaven.

Blazing like fire,
like sun,
like radiance beyond measure.

Arjuna saw no beginning.

No middle.

No end.

Only immeasurable power
stretching beyond thought.

The sun and moon
were His eyes.

Fire poured
from His mouths.

The whole universe
seemed scorched
by His brightness.

And Arjuna understood:

You are the imperishable.

You are the final shelter
of all worlds.

You are the guardian
of the eternal way.

You are the ancient Person,
the One who remains
when everything else passes.

But awe began
to turn into trembling.

The worlds shook
before this terrible beauty.

Gods entered into Him.

Sages praised Him.

Celestial beings stood astonished.

And Arjuna,
who had spoken with Krishna
as friend,
now found himself undone.

I see Your vast form,
he said.

Mouths without number.

Eyes without number.

Arms,
feet,
bellies,
faces everywhere.

Terrible teeth.

Blazing mouths.

The worlds are afraid.

And so am I.

My heart is shaken.

I cannot find steadiness.

I cannot find peace.

Then the vision became
more dreadful still.

Arjuna saw warriors
rushing into Krishna’s flaming mouths.

Kings.

Heroes.

Teachers.

Friends.

Enemies.

All being drawn
toward destruction.

Like rivers
running helplessly
toward the sea.

Like moths
flying into fire.

All the strength of the world,
all its pride,
all its armies,
all its certainty,
vanishing into the mouth
of the Eternal.

Arjuna lost all sense
of direction.

The battlefield
was no longer merely a battlefield.

It was time itself.

It was mortality unveiled.

It was the terrifying truth
that everything born
is already moving
toward dissolution.

And trembling,
Arjuna cried out:

Be merciful to me,
O shelter of the universe.

### Along the Way

This passage is difficult
because it refuses to give us
a small and manageable vision
of the Divine.

Many of us want the sacred
to be only comforting.

Only tender.

Only affirming.

And there is tenderness
at the heart of the spiritual life.

But the Gita also shows us
that ultimate reality
is not sentimental.

The Divine is not merely
a helper within our plans.

The Divine is the ground
beneath birth and death,
creation and destruction,
beauty and terror,
mercy and judgment,
beginning and end.

To encounter this
is to have our illusions stripped away.

Arjuna sees what most of us
spend our lives trying not to see:

everything passes.

Power passes.

Youth passes.

Achievement passes.

Reputation passes.

Even those we admire,
fear,
resent,
or depend upon
are mortal.

This is not easy wisdom.

It can feel overwhelming.

But it can also awaken us.

When we remember death,
we begin to see life more truthfully.

We stop pretending
that control is absolute.

We stop treating our conflicts
as though they are eternal.

We stop mistaking our temporary roles
for our deepest identity.

The ego fears this
because the ego survives
by pretending it can secure itself.

But the soul can receive it
as liberation.

Not because death is good in itself,
or because suffering should be minimized,
but because truth frees us
from living in fantasy.

The vision also reminds us
that awe and fear
are close neighbors.

Some truths are too large
to be absorbed calmly.

Sometimes the heart trembles
because it is standing
before reality
without its usual defenses.

And even then,
Arjuna does the most human thing.

He asks for mercy.

He does not explain himself.

He does not pretend bravery.

He simply cries out
from the place where awe
has become too much to bear.

That too
is a form of prayer.

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“Hold me in truth”

Breathing out:
“Have mercy on my fear”
