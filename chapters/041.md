## 41 — The Face That Calms the Fear

> *Awe can awaken the soul.  
> But love teaches it how to rest.*

## The Song

Arjuna bowed
again and again.

From the front,
from behind,
from every direction,
he offered himself
before the One
who filled all things.

You are the ancient One,
he said.

The first and final shelter.

The knower,
the known,
and the refuge
beyond all endings.

You are air.

You are fire.

You are water.

You are death.

You are the moon.

You are the source
from which all beings arise.

Again and again,
I bow.

But as reverence deepened,
memory returned.

Arjuna remembered
the ordinary nearness.

The shared meals.

The laughter.

The rest.

The friendship.

The casual words spoken
without understanding
the fullness
of the One beside him.

O Krishna,
O friend,
he had said so many times.

And now,
standing before immeasurable glory,
his heart trembled
with tenderness and regret.

Forgive me,
he said.

For every careless word.

For every familiar gesture.

For every moment
I treated You lightly,
not knowing Your greatness.

Forgive me
as a father forgives a child.

As a friend forgives a friend.

As one beloved
forgives another.

The vision had filled him
with wonder,
but also with fear.

So Arjuna asked
for a gentler sight.

Show me again
the form my heart can bear.

Let me see You
with crown and disc and club.

Let me see You
near enough
for love.

And Krishna answered
with compassion.

Do not be afraid.

Do not be confused.

Be peaceful again.

Then the terrible vastness
softened.

The blazing universal form
withdrew.

And Krishna showed Arjuna
His beautiful form once more.

The friend returned.

The guide returned.

The familiar face
through which the Infinite
had chosen to draw near.

And Arjuna breathed again.

Now my mind is steady,
he said.

Now I have come back
to myself.

### Along the Way

This passage reveals
the strange mercy
of nearness.

Arjuna has seen the universe
inside the Divine.

He has seen time,
death,
glory,
judgment,
beauty,
terror,
and infinity.

But after all that,
what restores him
is the face he knows.

The approachable presence.

The beloved form.

The One who can be spoken to,
trusted,
and loved.

There is wisdom here.

The soul needs awe.

Without awe,
we shrink reality
down to the size
of our own desires.

We become casual
with what is holy.

We forget reverence.

But awe alone
is not enough.

If the sacred remains only vast,
terrifying,
and unapproachable,
the heart cannot rest.

It may bow,
but it cannot love freely.

It may tremble,
but it cannot draw near.

The deepest spiritual life
holds both together:

reverence and intimacy.

Wonder and trust.

Holy fear
and tender love.

Arjuna’s apology
is not self-hatred.

It is awakening.

He realizes
that familiarity
can become forgetfulness.

We can stand near love
and fail to recognize it.

We can receive grace daily
and treat it as ordinary.

We can speak carelessly
to the very presence
that sustains us.

Yet the response is not rejection.

Krishna does not shame Arjuna.

He calms him.

He gives him back
the form his heart can receive.

This is a deeply healing image.

The Infinite does not demand
that the human soul remain overwhelmed.

The Holy knows our limits.

The One beyond all worlds
can come near enough
to steady our breathing.

And perhaps this is where devotion matures:

when we learn to bow
before mystery,
yet also allow ourselves
to be loved by it.

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“Steady my trembling heart”

Breathing out:
“Draw near in love”
