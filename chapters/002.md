## 2 — The Field Within

> *The battle begins long before the first arrow is released.  
> Every heart contains a field where fear, loyalty, desire, and conscience struggle for dominion.*

## The Song

The king could not see the battlefield with his own eyes.

Blindness had already settled over him long before the war began.

So he asked another to tell him what was unfolding on the sacred field of Kurukshetra, where two families stood facing one another beneath the weight of destiny.

“What are they doing now?” he asked.

The question trembled beneath the surface.

Not merely:
Who will win?

But:
What becomes of us when conflict can no longer be avoided?

Across the plain, warriors gathered in great formations.

Standards lifted into the wind.
Armor flashed beneath the morning light.
Conches and bows waited in silence.

Names were spoken one after another like echoes rolling across history.

Teachers.
Fathers.
Sons.
Friends.
Kings.
Students who had once learned side by side now stood divided by allegiance and ambition.

Duryodhana looked upon the opposing army and felt unease rise within him.

Though surrounded by power, he could not rest.

He counted warriors.
Measured strength.
Named weapons.
Spoke of loyalty.
Promised victory.

Yet beneath his confidence moved fear.

Again and again he searched for reassurance.

Protect this leader.
Strengthen this position.
Hold the line.

The battlefield outside mirrored the battlefield within.

Every army carried its own hunger.
Every warrior carried memory, pride, grief, longing, and attachment into the coming storm.

And the place where they gathered was called holy.

Not because violence itself was sacred,
but because truth would soon be uncovered there.

On that field, hidden motives would rise into the light.

Illusion would begin to crack.

The soul would no longer be able to avoid itself.

### Along the Way

Most human conflict begins long before words are spoken aloud.

Wars form quietly within the heart.

Fear hardens into defensiveness.
Attachment becomes possessiveness.
Woundedness seeks control.
The ego divides the world into “mine” and “theirs.”

Even noble causes can become tangled with pride, anxiety, or the need to preserve identity.

This opening scene reveals how deeply human beings seek certainty when standing before loss or change. Duryodhana surrounds himself with power, numbers, strategy, and loyal allies, yet still cannot find peace. The outer display of confidence hides inward instability.

Many of us live this way.

We try to quiet fear through control.
We gather accomplishments, status, arguments, possessions, or alliances hoping they will protect us from vulnerability.

But inner conflict cannot be healed through external dominance.

Peace is not merely the absence of struggle around us. It is the slow alignment of the heart with truth.

The sacred field is not only a distant battlefield from history.

It is the inward place where conscience awakens.

Where we begin to notice the motives beneath our actions.

Where we are invited to ask difficult questions:

What drives me?
What am I defending?
What fear rules me?
What would remain if I no longer needed to win?

The path toward wisdom often begins when our certainties begin to tremble.

Not to destroy us,
but to make honesty possible.

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“Quiet the battle within”

Breathing out:
“Teach me the way of peace”
