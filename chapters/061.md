## 61 — What Nourishes the Soul

> *The soul is shaped not only by what it believes,  
> but also by what it continually consumes.*

## The Song

Krishna spoke
of the quiet power
of daily life.

Not only prayer,
or worship,
or philosophy—

but food,
discipline,
and ordinary habits
shape the soul.

What we take into ourselves
becomes part of us.

Some foods,
He said,
bring vitality.

They strengthen the body,
steady the mind,
and increase joy,
clarity,
and health.

Freshness.

Wholesomeness.

Gentle nourishment.

These are loved
by those
moving toward harmony.

Other foods
overstimulate.

Too sharp.

Too intense.

Too excessive.

They inflame the body
and agitate the mind,
feeding restlessness,
dissatisfaction,
and imbalance.

Still other things
carry heaviness.

What is stale.

Decayed.

Lifeless.

Neglected.

The soul slowly dims
when it feeds continually
on what has lost its vitality.

Krishna’s teaching
reached beyond food itself.

Every form of consumption
shapes consciousness.

What we watch.

What we absorb.

What we repeat.

What we glorify.

What we crave.

All of it
quietly forms the inner world.

The spiritual life,
then,
is not only about dramatic revelations.

It is also about learning
to nourish the heart
with what brings life.

### Along the Way

This passage invites us
to pay attention
to something deeply practical:

what we regularly consume
affects who we become.

Modern life often separates
spirituality
from the body.

But Krishna speaks
as though body,
mind,
and soul
are deeply connected.

The food we eat,
the pace we live,
the atmosphere we surround ourselves with—
all influence consciousness.

This insight reaches far
beyond diet alone.

Some things nourish peace.

Other things nourish agitation.

Some habits
make us clearer,
gentler,
and more grounded.

Others leave us anxious,
numb,
or emotionally inflamed.

And much of modern culture
is built around overstimulation.

Constant noise.

Constant scrolling.

Constant craving.

Too much information.

Too much speed.

Too much intensity.

The nervous system
rarely rests.

The soul becomes exhausted
without fully understanding why.

Krishna’s teaching
is not rooted in shame
or rigid perfectionism.

It is rooted in awareness.

Notice what increases life.

Notice what deadens it.

Notice what brings clarity.

Notice what leaves you fragmented.

This kind of attentiveness
is itself a spiritual practice.

Many people
try to transform their lives
through dramatic effort,
while ignoring
the quiet shaping power
of daily rhythms.

But small things matter deeply.

A nourishing meal.

Silence instead of constant noise.

Rest instead of endless stimulation.

Conversations rooted in kindness.

Beauty.

Stillness.

Gratitude.

Over time,
the soul begins to reflect
what it repeatedly feeds upon.

And slowly,
almost imperceptibly,
peace grows.

Not through force.

But through nourishment.

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“Nourish me with what gives life”

Breathing out:
“Help my soul grow in peace”
