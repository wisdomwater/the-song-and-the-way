## 63 — The Spirit of Discipline

> *A practice that does not deepen love, gentleness, and humility  
> has missed the heart of the path.*

## The Song

Krishna spoke
of discipline.

Of fasting,
speech,
charity,
silence,
and self-restraint.

But He warned
that not all discipline
leads toward wisdom.

Some forms of austerity
soften the heart.

Others harden it.

Some practices
create compassion.

Others feed pride.

There are those,
He said,
who perform spiritual acts
to be admired.

To appear holy.

To gain prestige,
attention,
or influence.

Their discipline
becomes theatre.

The ego
wearing sacred clothing.

And there are others
who torment themselves
through harshness,
violence,
or foolish extremes.

Hurting the body.

Hurting others.

Mistaking suffering
for holiness.

But true discipline,
Krishna taught,
is gentler than this.

It is found
in sincerity.

In cleanliness of heart.

In truthful speech
that does not wound.

In self-control
without cruelty.

In kindness.

In reverence.

In inner steadiness.

Even silence,
He said,
can become sacred
when it arises
from peace
instead of resentment.

Even speech
can become an offering
when it is truthful,
helpful,
and healing.

And charity given
without seeking repayment,
recognition,
or advantage
carries the fragrance
of goodness.

The deepest practices
do not inflate the self.

They make the self
more transparent
to love.

### Along the Way

One of the great temptations
of spiritual life
is the belief
that harshness equals holiness.

Human beings often imagine
that transformation
must be severe,
dramatic,
or punishing.

We may secretly believe
that God is most pleased
when we exhaust ourselves,
deny ourselves violently,
or prove our worth
through suffering.

But Krishna challenges
this assumption.

Discipline is not meant
to destroy the self.

It is meant
to free the self.

Any practice
that deepens pride,
self-hatred,
judgment,
or cruelty
has drifted away
from wisdom.

True spiritual maturity
does not make a person
more rigid.

It makes them
more gentle,
more honest,
more compassionate,
and more awake.

This teaching feels especially important
in a world
obsessed with image.

Even spirituality
can become performance.

People curate holiness
the way others curate success.

Public virtue.

Visible sacrifice.

Carefully displayed morality.

But Krishna keeps returning
to the hidden interior.

Why are you doing this?

What spirit
moves beneath the action?

The invitation here
is not toward perfectionism.

It is toward sincerity.

Toward practices
that slowly heal
rather than practices
that violently control.

Sometimes
the holiest discipline
is not grand at all.

Speaking kindly.

Eating with awareness.

Listening without defensiveness.

Choosing honesty.

Resting when exhausted.

Remaining gentle
in a harsh world.

These too
are sacred acts.

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“Teach me the way of sincerity”

Breathing out:
“Let love shape my practice”
