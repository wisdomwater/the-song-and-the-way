## 21 — The Light Behind the Eyes

> *There is a quiet joy beneath the noise of craving.  
> A deeper life waits beneath the surface self.*

## The Song

Krishna spoke of the inner freedom
that begins when a person no longer believes
they are the center of every action.

The awakened one still moves through the world —
speaking,
working,
breathing,
choosing.

Yet inwardly,
they rest.

Like someone sitting peacefully
inside a house with many windows,
watching life move through its openings
without becoming trapped by every passing thing.

The body acts.
The senses encounter the world.
Nature unfolds according to its patterns.

But the deepest self
remains spacious and untouched.

Human beings become lost,
Krishna said,
because wisdom is covered over.

Like the sun hidden behind thick clouds.

Ignorance creates separation.

It teaches people to cling,
to compare,
to possess,
to fear.

And under this veil,
they forget what they truly are.

But when self-knowledge begins to rise,
it breaks through darkness
like dawn breaking across the earth.

Suddenly,
the soul sees differently.

Not merely with the eyes,
but with the heart.

The wise person no longer measures beings
according to status,
appearance,
wealth,
education,
or usefulness.

They see the same sacred life
moving through all forms.

The learned teacher.
The laborer.
The animal.
The outcast.
The stranger.

The outer forms differ.

But the inner light is shared.

And because of this,
the awakened person gradually becomes steady.

Pleasure comes —
they do not cling.

Pain comes —
they do not collapse.

Praise arrives —
they are not intoxicated.

Loss arrives —
they are not destroyed.

A deeper center begins forming within them.

Krishna said that those
who seek happiness only through external pleasures
will eventually suffer.

Because every pleasure born from grasping
contains its own ending.

The senses flare brightly for a moment,
then fade.

Desire promises satisfaction,
yet often leaves restlessness behind.

So the wise begin turning inward.

Not rejecting life,
but no longer demanding
that temporary things carry the weight
of ultimate fulfillment.

Within this inward turning,
another kind of joy quietly appears.

A joy not dependent on possession.

Not dependent on victory.

Not dependent on constant stimulation.

A quiet radiance.

An inner light.

And the one who learns
to remain present through desire,
fear,
and anger —
without being ruled by them —
begins tasting freedom
even before death.

### Along the Way

This chapter speaks deeply
to the exhaustion of the modern soul.

Many people spend their lives
searching for stability
through achievement,
consumption,
recognition,
or emotional intensity.

Yet even beautiful experiences
cannot permanently satisfy the deeper hunger within us.

The Gita does not condemn pleasure.

It simply observes its limitation.

Every experience rises and passes.

Every possession fades.

Every emotional high eventually softens.

And when we build our identity
upon unstable things,
anxiety naturally follows.

So much suffering comes
from trying to force permanence
out of what was never meant to last.

The spiritual path begins changing us
when we stop asking the world
to carry burdens it cannot hold.

This does not make life empty.

Strangely,
it often makes life more beautiful.

Because we begin receiving moments
as gifts
instead of clinging to them
as guarantees.

The chapter also offers
a profound vision of human equality.

The awakened person learns to see beyond surfaces.

Beyond class.

Beyond appearance.

Beyond social value.

Beyond usefulness.

This kind of seeing
is not sentimental.

It arises from recognizing
that every person carries hidden wounds,
longings,
fears,
and sacred depth.

The same breath moves through all.

The same longing for peace lives quietly
inside every heart.

And perhaps this is why humility
becomes so central to spiritual maturity.

The ego constantly ranks and separates.

But wisdom softens the need
to stand above others.

The chapter also speaks honestly
about anger,
fear,
and desire.

These forces are not eliminated instantly.

But the mature soul gradually learns
not to become possessed by them.

A space opens
between impulse and action.

Between emotion and identity.

And within that space,
freedom quietly grows.

The Gita ultimately points toward
an inward life
that is neither withdrawn nor numb,
but deeply awake.

A life rooted in something
that cannot be taken away by success or failure,
gain or loss,
pleasure or pain.

A life lit from within.

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“There is light within me”

Breathing out:
“I rest beneath the noise”
