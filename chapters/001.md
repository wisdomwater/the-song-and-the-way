## 1 — Auspicious Invocation

> *The path begins with remembrance.  
> Light is received through those who carry it faithfully.*

### The Song

I bow before the teacher who opened my eyes.

I was lost in a darkness I did not understand.
My thoughts moved blindly through the world.
But through the light of wisdom,
a lamp was placed into my hands.

I offer my gratitude to the lineage of those who carried this light forward.

To Rūpa Gosvāmī,
who devoted his life to fulfilling the longing of Śrī Caitanya.

To Sanātana.
To Raghunātha.
To Jīva.
To the companions and caretakers of devotion.

I bow before the Vaiṣṇavas,
whose lives became vessels of mercy.

I remember Advaita Ācārya,
Nityānanda Prabhu,
and Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu,
surrounded by devotees whose hearts were given fully to love.

I remember Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa,
and the gopīs of Vṛndāvana,
whose devotion moved like music through the forests and riverbanks.

By the grace of the teacher,
the holy name entered the heart.
The places of longing became alive:
Mathurā,
Vṛndāvana,
Govardhana,
Rādhā-kuṇḍa.

Not merely locations upon the earth,
but inner landscapes of love and remembrance.

I offer respect to Bhaktivedānta Swami Prabhupāda,
who carried these teachings across oceans,
bringing the song of devotion into distant lands.

Taking the instruction of his teacher upon his head,
he crossed cultures and languages
to awaken forgotten hearts.

I bow also to Bhakti Rakṣaka Śrīdhara Deva Gosvāmī,
described as an ocean of mercy,
who gave light to those still wandering in ignorance
and taught the deep mystery of detachment joined with love.

Again and again the prayers return to mercy.

The Vaiṣṇavas are called desire trees,
offering shelter to weary souls.
They are described as oceans,
lifting others from suffering and confusion.

Then the invocation turns toward Caitanya and Nityānanda,
rising together like the sun and moon,
driving away the darkness of the age.

The prayers speak of divine love poured freely into the world.

Of Gadādhara,
giver of spiritual strength.

Of the many forms devotion takes:
the servant,
the companion,
the incarnation,
the energy of love itself.

Then the heart cries out directly:

“O Kṛṣṇa,
ocean of mercy,
friend of the distressed,
beloved of the humble,
I bow before You.”

The images become luminous.

Rādhā and Govinda seated beneath wish-fulfilling trees.
Jewelled forests.
Flute music calling through the night.
The golden radiance of Rādhārāṇī.
The sacred tenderness of Tulasī.

Even Narasiṁha appears —
fierce,
burning,
terrible against death itself,
yet protective toward the devoted heart.

And at last,
all words give way to chant.

The holy names rise and fall like breathing:

Hare Kṛṣṇa Hare Kṛṣṇa
Kṛṣṇa Kṛṣṇa Hare Hare

Hare Rāma Hare Rāma
Rāma Rāma Hare Hare

The mind quiets.

The heart remembers.

The song begins.

### Along the Way

These opening invocations reveal a spirituality rooted in relationship, gratitude, and remembrance.

The soul does not awaken through information alone. It awakens through encounter — through people whose lives become windows into compassion, humility, wisdom, and love.

The repeated naming of teachers and saints may feel unfamiliar to modern readers, yet there is something deeply human beneath it: the recognition that transformation is carried through living hearts.

No one finds the way entirely alone.

Someone teaches us how to pray.
Someone shows us patience.
Someone embodies gentleness in a world shaped by fear.
Someone carries hope long enough for us to begin believing again.

The prayers also reveal a different vision of greatness.

The honored figures are remembered not for domination or achievement, but for devotion, mercy, surrender, and service. Their authority flows from love rather than control.

Again and again the text returns to the movement from blindness into sight.

This blindness is not merely intellectual.
It is the confusion of the ego —
the tendency to mistake possession for fulfillment,
performance for worth,
certainty for wisdom.

The path begins when the heart becomes willing to be taught.

There is also a profound tenderness in the repeated cry for mercy.

The human soul grows tired.
Distracted.
Fragmented.
Burdened by longing and fear.

Yet the invocation insists that compassion continues to move toward the weary.

Light still seeks the lost.

And perhaps this is why the prayers end not in explanation, but in chant.

Some truths are not solved by analysis.

They must be breathed.
Repeated.
Remembered
until they descend from the mind into the heart.

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“Open my darkened eyes”

Breathing out:
“Lead me into love”
