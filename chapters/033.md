## 33 — The Offering of the Heart

> *Nothing given in love is too small.  
> The smallest offering can become holy.*

## The Song

Krishna spoke
of the many things
people chase.

Pleasure.

Reward.

Recognition.

Even heaven itself.

Some perform sacred rituals
hoping to reach higher realms.

Some long for celestial joy,
for lives untouched by suffering,
for reward beyond the limits
of ordinary human existence.

And for a time,
they may taste such things.

But every reward
that depends upon achievement
eventually fades.

Even heavenly pleasures pass away.

Even spiritual ambition
can become another form
of grasping.

The soul rises.

The soul returns.

Again and again.

But those who turn toward the Divine
with an undivided heart
are held differently.

Krishna said:

For those who remain close to Me,
who remember Me,
who live in loving devotion,
I carry what they lack
and preserve what they have.

The path became suddenly intimate.

No longer distant.

No longer reserved
for the spiritually impressive.

If one offers Me
a leaf,
a flower,
a piece of fruit,
or water
with sincerity,
I accept it.

Not because of its value.

Not because of its rarity.

But because love
has touched it.

And Krishna said:

Whatever you do,
whatever you eat,
whatever you give,
whatever sacrifice you make,
offer it to Me.

Let your whole life
become an offering.

Then even ordinary actions
begin to change.

Eating.

Working.

Speaking.

Serving.

Giving.

Suffering.

All of it
can become sacred
when surrendered in love.

And Krishna spoke
with startling tenderness:

I am equal toward all beings.

I reject no one.

And even the one
who has wandered far,
who has failed deeply,
who has lived in confusion,
if they turn toward Me
with wholehearted devotion,
they are not lost.

Such a soul
is already turning toward peace.

For no one
who sincerely seeks the Divine
is abandoned.

### Along the Way

This passage moves spirituality
out of the realm
of performance
and into the realm
of relationship.

Human beings naturally measure worth.

We compare.

We rank.

We imagine that holiness belongs
to the disciplined,
the accomplished,
the morally impressive,
or the spiritually gifted.

But the Gita keeps returning
to something simpler
and more difficult:

the orientation of the heart.

A small act done in love
carries immense weight.

A large act done for ego
can remain empty.

This changes how we understand devotion.

Devotion is not merely ritual activity.

It is the gradual offering
of the self.

The ego loosens its grip.

Possessiveness softens.

Control softens.

Life slowly becomes less centered
around “What do I gain?”
and more centered around
“How do I live in alignment
with love?”

The passage also offers
a deeply compassionate vision
of transformation.

People fail.

People become lost.

People act from fear,
wounding,
confusion,
addiction,
anger,
and selfishness.

The spiritual life does not deny this.

But neither does it reduce a person
to their worst moment.

Again and again,
the Gita insists
that sincere turning matters.

The heart can change direction.

The soul can awaken.

Peace can grow
even after long wandering.

This does not excuse harm.

But it does refuse despair.

No life is beyond redemption
when love becomes genuine.

And perhaps this is part
of the great invitation here:

Do not wait
until you become perfect
to begin offering yourself.

Begin now.

With what you have.

With who you are.

With one small sincere act
at a time.

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“Receive this simple offering”

Breathing out:
“Teach my heart to love”
