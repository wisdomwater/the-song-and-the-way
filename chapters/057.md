## 57 — The Two Movements of the Heart

> *Every heart bends toward either love or self-importance.  
> The soul becomes what it repeatedly practices.*

## The Song

Krishna spoke
of two movements
within human nature.

One movement
opens the heart.

The other
closes it.

The first carries
the fragrance
of the divine.

Fearlessness.

Sincerity.

Self-restraint.

Compassion.

Truthfulness.

Gentleness.

Patience.

A heart unwilling
to harm others.

A spirit
less consumed
with possession and prestige.

The soul shaped
by these qualities
becomes peaceful.

Clear.

Softened by wisdom.

Such a person
does not delight
in humiliating others.

Does not constantly search
for faults.

Does not build identity
through domination.

There is humility
within them.

A quiet steadiness.

A willingness
to love without demanding
constant reward.

Then Krishna spoke
of another movement.

Pride.

Arrogance.

Harshness.

Anger.

Cruelty.

Self-importance.

A life governed
by ego and ignorance.

The heart closes inward.

Other people become objects
to control,
compete with,
or use.

Compassion weakens.

Restlessness grows.

The soul becomes trapped
inside itself.

Krishna said
these two movements
lead in different directions.

One toward freedom.

One toward bondage.

And He spoke gently
to Arjuna:

Do not be afraid.

Your heart
is meant for the path
of awakening.

### Along the Way

This passage feels remarkably relevant
to human life.

Krishna is not dividing people
into simplistic categories
of “good” and “bad.”

He is describing
two directions
the human heart can move.

One direction
opens toward humility,
compassion,
truth,
and inner freedom.

The other contracts
around ego,
fear,
power,
and self-centeredness.

Every person
contains both possibilities.

And often,
both movements
exist within us
at the same time.

We can be generous one moment
and defensive the next.

Tender one day
and harsh another.

The spiritual life
is not about pretending
we have no darkness.

It is about becoming honest enough
to see what we are feeding.

Krishna’s list
of divine qualities
is strikingly practical.

Compassion.

Patience.

Freedom from constant fault-finding.

Gentleness.

Self-control.

These are not abstract ideals.

They shape
how we treat real people.

How we speak.

How we respond under pressure.

How we carry power.

How we handle disappointment.

The qualities of ego,
by contrast,
always create separation.

Pride isolates.

Arrogance hardens.

Anger distorts perception.

Cruelty deadens the heart.

And much of modern culture
quietly rewards these traits.

Dominance is praised.

Humility is mistaken for weakness.

Aggression is celebrated.

Attention becomes more valuable
than wisdom.

But Krishna points
toward another kind of strength.

The strength
to remain gentle
without becoming passive.

The strength
to remain truthful
without cruelty.

The strength
to resist becoming consumed
by ego and resentment.

Most transformation
does not happen dramatically.

It happens slowly,
through repeated choices.

What we practice,
we become.

And every act of compassion,
every restraint of anger,
every movement toward humility,
quietly reshapes the soul.

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“Shape my heart with compassion”

Breathing out:
“Free me from pride”
