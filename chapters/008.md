## 8 — Beneath What Changes

> *Everything visible passes through seasons of appearing and disappearing.  
> Yet beneath the changing forms of life, something eternal quietly remains.*

## The Song

Krishna continued speaking into the trembling silence.

The body changes.
The world changes.
Everything touched by time rises and fades like mist beneath the morning sun.

Yet the deepest self remains untouched.

It cannot be cut by weapons.
Cannot be consumed by fire.
Cannot be drowned by water or carried away by wind.

Invisible.
Ungraspable.
Beyond the reach of ordinary sight.

Still it lives.

Most people know themselves only through changing things.

Through the body.
Through memory.
Through pleasure and pain.
Through status and loss.
Through the fragile stories they build around temporary forms.

And because these things are unstable,
fear follows them everywhere.

But Krishna spoke of a consciousness deeper than all appearances.

Ancient,
yet ever fresh.

Unborn,
yet always present.

Not trapped inside time,
though it moves through every season of human life.

Birth reveals it for a moment.
Death conceals it again.

Like waves rising briefly upon the surface of an endless sea.

Before birth,
unseen.

For a little while,
visible.

Then hidden once more.

Why then, Krishna asked gently,
should the passing form be mistaken for the whole of reality?

Some hear this truth and marvel.
Some speak of it beautifully.
Some spend lifetimes studying it.

And still many never truly understand.

For the mind clings fiercely to what can be touched.

The heart fears what it cannot control.

Yet the eternal waits quietly beneath both fear and forgetting.

Then Krishna turned again toward Arjuna’s trembling hesitation.

“You grieve because you see only the loss before you.

But you must also remember who you are.”

A warrior standing for righteousness cannot remain frozen forever between fear and responsibility.

There are moments when avoiding action wounds the soul as deeply as reckless action.

And so Krishna called Arjuna not toward hatred,
but toward alignment.

Toward action rooted in truth rather than attachment.

Toward courage free from selfish ambition.

### Along the Way

Human beings spend enormous energy trying to preserve what cannot ultimately be preserved.

Youth fades.
Bodies weaken.
Relationships change.
Roles disappear.
Possessions scatter.
Even entire civilizations rise and fall.

Much suffering comes not simply from loss itself,
but from believing that temporary things were meant to give permanent security.

The wisdom here invites a deeper grounding.

Not in denial of the world,
but in remembering that life contains dimensions larger than what the senses alone can perceive.

There is something within human experience that longs for permanence because, at the deepest level, it senses eternity.

This longing appears in our fear of death,
our resistance to change,
our desire to be remembered,
our ache for enduring love.

The text invites us to look beneath surface identity and rediscover the deeper life moving through us all.

This does not make grief meaningless.

We still mourn.
We still love.
We still feel the pain of separation.

But grief softens when we no longer believe that death or change has the final word over reality itself.

At the same time, Krishna introduces another difficult truth:

Avoiding responsibility can become its own form of attachment.

Spirituality is not escape from life.
It is not passive withdrawal from difficult moral action.

There are moments when love asks for courage.
Moments when conscience asks us to stand firm even while the heart trembles.

The challenge is learning how to act without hatred.

To move without losing compassion.
To remain grounded in truth without becoming consumed by ego, fear, or vengeance.

This is the beginning of awakened action.

Not driven by self-protection,
but guided by something deeper than the restless demands of the temporary self.

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“What is eternal remains”

Breathing out:
“Help me live without fear”
