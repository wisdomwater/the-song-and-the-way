## 20 — The Lotus in the Water

> *Freedom is not found by escaping life,  
> but by no longer clinging to it.*

## The Song

Arjuna was still wrestling within himself.

Again he asked Krishna:

Which path is truly higher?

To leave the world behind —
or to remain within it,
acting without attachment?

Krishna answered gently:

Both paths can lead toward liberation.

But the path of selfless action
is easier for most souls.

True renunciation,
He said,
is not merely withdrawing from activity.

It is the relinquishing
of grasping,
hatred,
and craving.

The real renunciate
is not the one who abandons the world,
but the one who abandons possessiveness.

Then Krishna revealed something subtle:

The paths of wisdom and action
are not ultimately separate.

The mature soul discovers
that contemplation and loving action
flow together.

The awakened person continues moving through life —
seeing,
hearing,
touching,
speaking,
walking,
eating,
breathing.

Yet inwardly,
they understand:

“I am not the isolated doer.”

The senses move among the world.
Life unfolds through countless forces.
But the deepest self remains untouched.

Like a lotus resting upon water,
yet unstained by it.

Such a person works,
but is not imprisoned by work.

Serves,
without becoming consumed by reward.

Acts,
without fastening identity
to success or failure.

The selfish mind clings tightly
to outcomes.

It fears loss.
It craves recognition.
It anxiously calculates worth.

But the karma-yogi gradually releases this burden.

And in releasing it,
peace begins to settle within.

### Along the Way

This chapter speaks directly into one of the deepest tensions
many people experience in spiritual life:

Do I withdraw from the world
to become whole?

Or do I remain engaged?

Many traditions have wrestled with this question.

Some people imagine spirituality
as escape from ordinary responsibilities.

Others become so immersed in productivity,
service,
or activism
that they lose all interior rootedness.

But the Gita offers a third way.

It does not glorify passivity.

Nor does it glorify frantic striving.

Instead,
it teaches participation without possession.

This is profoundly difficult.

Because the ego instinctively builds identity
through outcomes.

“If I succeed, I matter.”
“If I fail, I am diminished.”
“If others admire me, I am safe.”
“If they reject me, I disappear.”

Most suffering comes from this hidden entanglement.

Not merely from action itself —
but from the self we construct around action.

The Gita repeatedly loosens this knot.

You are invited to act fully,
love deeply,
serve faithfully,
and engage honestly with life —
without making the fruits of those actions
the foundation of your worth.

This does not create indifference.

It creates freedom.

And perhaps the lotus image says it best.

The lotus grows directly from muddy water.

It does not deny the mud.
It does not flee the water.

Yet somehow,
it remains untouched by what surrounds it.

The spiritual life is not always about leaving difficult conditions.

Sometimes it is about becoming inwardly unbound within them.

The Gita also gently dismantles the illusion
that we are isolated “doers.”

This can sound strange at first.

But consider how little of existence we actually control.

The body breathes.
The heart beats.
Thoughts arise.
Emotions move.
Weather shifts.
History unfolds.
Relationships shape us.
Grace appears unexpectedly.

Life is deeply participatory.

The awakened person begins acting with humility,
recognizing that they are part of something vastly larger
than the small ego-self.

And strangely,
this realization does not weaken action.

It purifies it.

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“Teach me to act without clinging”

Breathing out:
“Let me rest like the lotus”
