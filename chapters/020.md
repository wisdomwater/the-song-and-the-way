## 20 - The Lotus in the Water

> *Freedom is not found by escaping life, but by no longer clinging to it.*

Many people imagine peace exists somewhere far from ordinary life.

Away from conflict.
Away from responsibility.
Away from the endless demands of work, relationships, grief, uncertainty, and human limitation.

The soul dreams of escape.

“If only I could leave all this behind,” it says, “then perhaps I would finally become whole.”

Yet the deeper wisdom traditions often point somewhere more subtle.

Freedom is not always found by withdrawing from life.

Sometimes it is discovered by learning how to remain inwardly unbound while fully participating in it.

This is far more difficult than it first appears.

Because the ego fastens itself constantly to outcomes.

To reputation.
To success.
To being understood.
To controlling how life unfolds.

And once identity becomes entangled with these things, the inner world loses its steadiness.

Praise lifts us briefly.
Criticism wounds us deeply.
Success becomes intoxicating.
Failure becomes unbearable.

The soul begins living at the mercy of circumstances.

But there is another way of being.

A quieter way.

To move through life sincerely,
while loosening the compulsive need to possess it.

To work wholeheartedly,
without making achievement the foundation of worth.

To love deeply,
without trying to control or imprison what is loved.

To remain present within sorrow,
without becoming entirely defined by suffering.

The ancient image of the lotus speaks gently here.

The lotus grows directly from muddy water.

It does not deny the mud.
It does not wait for perfect conditions before opening.

And yet somehow, it remains unstained.

This is not detachment in the cold or indifferent sense.

The lotus still touches the water.
It simply does not drown within it.

So too the awakened heart learns to remain in contact with the world without becoming consumed by every passing wave.

This kind of freedom does not appear dramatic outwardly.

A person still works.
Still cooks meals.
Still answers difficult emails.
Still cares for aging parents.
Still feels grief and disappointment and uncertainty.

But inwardly, something has changed.

The constant grasping begins to soften.

The soul no longer demands that life arrange itself perfectly in order for peace to exist.

This creates a different kind of presence.

Less reactive.
Less defensive.
Less desperate to prove oneself through endless activity.

There is also deep humility hidden within this path.

For slowly, a person begins realizing how much of life was never fully under their control to begin with.

The heart beats without command.
Thoughts arise mysteriously.
Weather changes.
People make choices we cannot govern.
Love arrives unexpectedly.
Loss does too.

Life unfolds through countless movements larger than the small self.

And strangely, recognizing this can become liberating.

Not because responsibility disappears,
but because the crushing illusion of total control begins to loosen.

The soul no longer has to carry the unbearable burden of being the center of the universe.

It can participate instead.

Like the lotus resting quietly upon the water,
held without clinging,
present without fear,
open without possession.

And perhaps this is where peace finally begins —
not in escaping life,
but in learning how to remain spacious within it.

### Breath Prayer

> Inhale: *Teach me to act without clinging*
>
> Exhale: *Let me rest like the lotus*
