## 42 — The Way of Devotion

> *The Infinite may be contemplated from afar.  
> But love draws the heart near.*

## The Song

Krishna spoke
after the vision had passed.

This form,
He said,
cannot be seen
through study alone.

Not through rituals.

Not through severe disciplines.

Not through gifts
or sacrifice
or the strength
of human striving.

Arjuna had seen
because grace had opened the way.

And Krishna made the path clear:

Only through undivided devotion
can I be truly known.

Only through love
can I be seen
as I am.

Only through surrender
can the soul enter
into the mystery
it longs to behold.

The great vision
had not ended in spectacle.

It ended in relationship.

Not in power.

Not in achievement.

Not in spiritual display.

But in devotion
free from malice,
free from grasping,
free from hostility
toward any living being.

The one who serves,
who makes the Eternal
their highest aim,
who releases possessiveness,
who harms no creature
in the heart —
such a one comes to Me.

Then Arjuna asked
the question many seekers ask.

Which way is best?

The way of personal devotion,
where the heart turns toward You
with love?

Or the way of the formless,
the imperishable,
the unseen beyond all images?

Krishna answered with clarity.

Those who fix their minds on Me,
who remain joined to Me,
who live in trust and devotion —
these I consider most deeply united.

Yet He did not reject
the seekers of the formless.

Those who discipline the senses,
remain even-minded,
serve the welfare of all beings,
and contemplate the unmanifest
also come near.

But the path is harder.

For embodied beings,
the unseen without form
is difficult to hold.

The human heart
needs nearness.

The soul needs a face
toward which love can move.

### Along the Way

This passage gently brings us
from overwhelming vision
back into the simplicity
of devotion.

After the universal form,
one might expect Krishna
to emphasize spiritual power,
secret knowledge,
or extraordinary experience.

Instead,
He emphasizes love.

This is important.

The spiritual life can easily become
another form of ambition.

We may want visions.

Certainty.

Mystical experiences.

Proof that we are progressing.

But the Gita keeps returning
to the transformation of the heart.

Can we love without possessing?

Can we serve without needing praise?

Can we release hatred?

Can we become free
from malice toward all beings?

Can we orient our lives
toward what is eternal
while remaining tender
toward what is fragile?

This is the measure
of devotion.

Arjuna’s question
about the personal and the formless
also speaks to a real human tension.

Some are drawn
toward silence,
mystery,
vastness,
and the nameless depth
beyond all images.

Others are drawn
toward relationship,
love,
beauty,
address,
and personal nearness.

The Gita honors both movements,
but it also acknowledges
something deeply human:

love needs somewhere to rest.

Embodied people
often need embodied devotion.

We need practices
that engage the heart,
the voice,
the imagination,
the hands,
the daily life.

An abstract ideal
can be beautiful,
but difficult to love
when the soul is weary.

A distant infinity
can inspire awe,
but not always intimacy.

The path of devotion
offers nearness.

It teaches us
to bring the mind back
again and again
to the Beloved.

To make work an offering.

To let kindness become worship.

To let freedom from hatred
become a sign
that love is becoming real.

And in this way,
the heart slowly learns
what the mind alone
could never master.

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“Draw my heart near”

Breathing out:
“Free me from all malice”
