## 62 — The Spirit Behind the Offering

> *What matters most is not outward performance,  
> but the love and consciousness carried within the act.*

## The Song

Krishna continued
speaking about nourishment,
devotion,
and the hidden motives
of the heart.

Even sacred acts,
He said,
carry different qualities
depending on the spirit
in which they are done.

Some offerings
are made quietly,
with sincerity.

Without demand for reward.

Without performance.

Without the hunger
to be admired.

Such acts
carry the fragrance
of goodness.

They arise
from reverence,
love,
and inner steadiness.

Other offerings
are made for display.

To gain recognition.

To appear spiritual.

To increase status,
power,
or approval.

Though dressed
in religious clothing,
the heart remains restless,
still bargaining for advantage.

And still others
are empty altogether.

Performed carelessly.

Without love.

Without awareness.

Without reverence
for life itself.

Krishna spoke also
of food prepared
with devotion.

Food offered
with gratitude.

Food received
as sacred nourishment,
not merely consumption.

The ordinary act
of eating
became transformed
into communion.

An offering.

A participation
in something holy.

The path of awakening,
Krishna taught,
is not merely about
what is done outwardly.

It is about
the consciousness
that fills the act.

The hidden intention.

The spirit behind the gesture.

### Along the Way

This passage gently exposes
how easily the ego
can hide itself
inside outward goodness.

Even generosity.

Even spirituality.

Even acts of service.

Human beings often long
to be seen as good,
wise,
or important.

And because of this,
the ego can quietly turn
sacred practices
into performance.

We begin to seek recognition
instead of transformation.

Approval
instead of sincerity.

Appearance
instead of love.

Krishna’s teaching
calls us back
to the interior life.

Why am I doing this?

What spirit
is moving beneath the action?

An outwardly beautiful act
can still be driven
by vanity,
fear,
or control.

And a very simple act—
offering a meal,
washing dishes,
listening with compassion—
can become deeply sacred
when done with presence and love.

This passage also reveals
a profoundly tender vision
of ordinary life.

Eating.

Cooking.

Sharing food.

These are not treated
as spiritually insignificant.

The sacred
is not confined
to temples,
rituals,
or dramatic experiences.

It enters kitchens.

Tables.

Conversations.

Daily rhythms.

A meal prepared with care
and received with gratitude
can nourish more
than the body.

It can soften the heart.

Slow the mind.

Restore connection.

Modern life often trains us
to consume unconsciously.

Quickly.

Distractedly.

Without reverence.

But Krishna invites
another way of living.

To receive life itself
as gift.

To approach ordinary moments
with awareness.

To let devotion become
less about performance
and more about presence.

Not asking constantly,
“How do I appear?”

But quietly asking instead:

“Am I becoming
more loving?”

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“Fill my actions with sincerity”

Breathing out:
“Teach me the way of love”
