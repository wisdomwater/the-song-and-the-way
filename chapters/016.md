## 16 - The One Who Keeps Returning

> *Love does not abandon the world when the world loses its way.*

There are moments when the world feels unbearably lost.

Violence grows louder.
Truth becomes difficult to recognize.
People wound one another while calling it righteousness.
The noise of distraction fills every quiet space.

And beneath it all, many souls carry a hidden exhaustion.

A longing for something real.
Something steady.
Something untouched by the endless cycles of fear, anger, and confusion.

The ancient wisdom traditions understood this ache well.

They spoke of a sacred presence that does not abandon humanity when darkness deepens.

Again and again, love moves toward the wounded world.

Not always dramatically.

Sometimes through teachers.
Sometimes through prophets.
Sometimes through acts of courage and mercy.
Sometimes through a quiet awakening inside an ordinary human heart.

The sacred keeps returning.

This is one of the great hopes woven through the spiritual life:
that reality is not indifferent to suffering.

Even after generations of forgetfulness,
something continues calling humanity back toward compassion,
back toward truth,
back toward remembrance.

This returning happens inwardly as well.

There are seasons when a person loses themselves.

The heart becomes buried beneath survival.
Beneath distraction.
Beneath bitterness or grief.
Beneath years spent performing strength while secretly feeling numb inside.

And yet, even there,
something remains alive beneath the surface.

A quiet pull toward goodness.
A tenderness that refuses to disappear completely.
A sudden longing for honesty after years of hiding.
A moment of stillness that breaks through inner noise like sunlight entering a darkened room.

The soul remembers in flashes.

Not because it has achieved perfection,
but because something deeper keeps calling it home.

This is why practices of remembrance matter.

Silence.
Prayer.
Breathing slowly.
Lighting a candle before dawn.
Singing softly in the dark.
Walking beneath trees without rushing.
Repeating sacred words until they descend from the mind into the heart.

These small acts gather the scattered self.

They interrupt forgetfulness.

The modern world trains attention to remain fragmented.
Pulled constantly outward.
Measured by speed, reaction, stimulation, and endless comparison.

But the deeper life grows differently.

Quietly.
Patiently.
Almost invisibly at first.

Like roots spreading underground before any green shoot appears above the soil.

Many people think spiritual transformation means becoming extraordinary.

Yet often it looks more like returning.

Returning to presence.
Returning to sincerity.
Returning to compassion.
Returning to the simple awareness that we belong to something larger than fear.

Again and again,
the sacred invites this return.

Not with condemnation,
but with patience.

Like a voice calling gently through fog.

Like a hand remaining extended long after we expected it to withdraw.

Perhaps grace is not the absence of wandering.

Perhaps grace is that love continues calling us home even after we have wandered many times before.

And perhaps the deepest wisdom is simply this:
to keep returning too.
