## 16 — The One Who Keeps Returning

> *Love does not abandon the world when the world loses its way.*

## The Song

Arjuna listened as Krishna spoke of ancient wisdom —
a path older than kingdoms,
older than memory,
older even than the rise and fall of civilizations.

“This yoga,” Krishna said,
“I first taught to the sun-god long ago.
It was passed from one generation to another,
from ruler to sage,
from teacher to student.

But over time,
the wisdom was forgotten.”

Arjuna was confused.

“How could this be?”
he asked.

“You stand before me now as my friend.
How could You have taught this in ages long past?”

And Krishna answered with mystery.

“Both you and I have lived many lives, Arjuna.

You have forgotten them.

I have not.”

Though unborn,
though eternal,
though beyond decay,
Krishna said He still enters the world again and again.

Not because He must.

But because compassion moves Him.

Whenever wisdom fades,
whenever injustice rises,
whenever human beings lose their way in violence,
greed,
confusion,
and spiritual forgetfulness —

He comes.

Age after age.

To protect the vulnerable.
To confront destructive forces.
To restore the path that leads back to truth.

Krishna described His appearances not as ordinary births,
but as divine manifestations —
acts of love entering history.

And He said:

“One who truly understands this
is no longer trapped in endless cycles of fear and rebirth,
but comes into union with Me.”

Then Krishna spoke of the age now unfolding —
an age of noise,
restlessness,
division,
and spiritual exhaustion.

An age where distraction multiplies endlessly.

An age where people search everywhere for fulfillment
yet rarely become still enough to hear the soul.

And for this age,
He offered a simple path:

Remember the Divine Name.

Sing it.
Speak it.
Return to it.

Again and again.

Not as empty ritual,
but as reorientation.

A way of turning the heart back toward love.

### Along the Way

One of the most beautiful ideas in the Gita is that the Divine does not remain distant from human suffering.

Whenever consciousness declines into cruelty,
confusion,
or despair,
the sacred moves toward humanity rather than away from it.

Different traditions express this differently.

Some speak of prophets.
Some of incarnations.
Some of awakened beings.
Some of divine intervention.
Some of Christ.
Some of Krishna.
Some of the Spirit moving through history.

But underneath these images is a shared intuition:

Reality is not indifferent.

Love keeps reaching toward us.

Again and again.

The Gita frames this through the recurring appearance of Krishna —
the Divine entering history to restore balance and awaken consciousness.

But there is also a deeply inward reading of these verses.

The Divine appears not only in history,
but within the soul itself.

Again and again,
something within us calls us back when we lose ourselves.

A quiet voice beneath the noise.

A pull toward truth.

A longing for goodness even after failure.

A sudden clarity after years of confusion.

A tenderness that survives cynicism.

The sacred keeps returning.

And perhaps this is why practices of remembrance matter so deeply.

The chanting of sacred names,
breath prayers,
silence,
meditation,
devotion,
worship,
contemplation —
all of these interrupt forgetfulness.

They help gather the scattered mind.

They return us to the deeper center beneath the chaos.

The Gita repeatedly describes spiritual life not as acquiring something foreign,
but remembering what has been forgotten.

The soul already belongs to the Divine.

It has simply fallen asleep beneath distraction,
fear,
ego,
and endless craving.

So the path becomes less about becoming extraordinary
and more about returning.

Returning to awareness.
Returning to love.
Returning to presence.
Returning to the deeper truth already waiting beneath the surface.

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“Call me back to truth”

Breathing out:
“When I lose my way”
