## 22 — The Friend Within

> *The mind can become a doorway into peace,  
> or the room where we keep ourselves imprisoned.*

## The Song

Krishna brought the teaching of renunciation
to a quiet resting place.

Peace comes, He said,
when the heart knows
that the Holy One is not distant,
not cold,
not merely ruler or witness,
but the friend of all beings.

The One who receives every sacrifice.

The One who holds every world.

The One whose nearness is not domination,
but care.

To know this
is to let the soul unclench.

For peace is difficult to find
when we believe everything rests upon us.

When we imagine ourselves
as the keeper of every outcome,
the judge of every failure,
the controller of every world.

But the heart softens
when it remembers:

I am not alone here.

Life is held by a Love
larger than my striving.

Then Krishna began again,
turning Arjuna toward the inner work of yoga.

The true renunciate,
He said,
is not the one who merely stops acting.

Not the one who withdraws from fire,
ritual,
duty,
or human responsibility.

The true yogi is the one
who does what must be done
without leaning on the fruit of action
for identity.

They act,
but do not cling.

They serve,
but do not possess.

They enter the work before them,
yet do not make reward
the measure of their soul.

For no one becomes free
while secretly feeding every desire.

No one becomes whole
while the mind remains captive
to endless self-satisfaction.

At first,
Krishna said,
action is the path.

The beginner learns through faithful doing.

Through practice.

Through discipline.

Through showing up
when the heart is still divided.

But as yoga deepens,
quietness becomes the path.

The soul begins to rest
from grasping.

Not from love.

Not from responsibility.

But from the restless need
to make life serve the ego.

Then Krishna spoke
one of the most searching truths:

The mind can lift a person upward,
or drag a person downward.

The mind can become a friend.

The mind can become an enemy.

When the mind is steadied,
it becomes a companion on the way.

It helps the soul remain calm
through heat and cold,
pleasure and pain,
honor and dishonor.

But when the mind is ungoverned,
it turns against the person who carries it.

It magnifies fear.

It feeds resentment.

It chases desire.

It rehearses insult.

It makes enemies out of shadows.

The one who has learned to quiet the mind
becomes inwardly steady.

Dirt,
stone,
and gold
no longer hold the power
to define what is real.

A friend,
an enemy,
a stranger,
a relative,
the honorable,
the broken,
the difficult,
the beloved —
all are seen with a deeper gaze.

Not because all actions are the same.

Not because wisdom has become blind.

But because the awakened heart
has begun to see beneath appearances.

It no longer lets preference alone
decide who is worthy of compassion.

### Along the Way

This passage gathers two movements together.

First,
it offers the soul a foundation for peace.

And then,
it shows the difficult inner work
required to live from that peace.

Peace begins when we stop pretending
we are the hidden center of all things.

So much anxiety grows
from the illusion of total control.

We try to manage every outcome,
protect every image,
solve every uncertainty,
and carry every world inside ourselves.

But the Gita invites a surrender
that is not passive or careless.

It is the surrender of false ownership.

The world is not held together
by our fear.

The deepest life is not sustained
by our striving.

There is a Friend of all beings.

To trust this
does not remove responsibility.

It purifies it.

We can act more freely
when we are no longer acting
to prove that we are enough.

Then the teaching turns inward.

The mind must be gently trained.

Not hated.

Not crushed.

Not shamed into silence.

Trained.

A mind left alone
often becomes a servant of fear and appetite.

It reaches for what cannot satisfy.

It creates stories out of pain.

It turns wounds into identities.

It clings to praise
and trembles before rejection.

But the mind can also become a friend.

When guided with patience,
truth,
and practice,
it becomes a place of steadiness.

A place where reaction slows down.

A place where wisdom has room to breathe.

This is where equality of vision begins.

Not as an idea,
but as a transformation of perception.

The mature soul still recognizes harm.

Still discerns what is wise and unwise.

Still understands that actions have consequences.

But beneath all this,
it sees a shared sacredness.

The person who blesses us.

The person who wounds us.

The person we admire.

The person we avoid.

The person praised by society.

The person forgotten by everyone.

All are held within the same mystery.

All are worthy of compassion.

This does not make the heart naive.

It makes the heart free.

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“Steady my restless mind”

Breathing out:
“Teach me to see with peace”
