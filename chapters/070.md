## 70 - The Work We Are Meant to Do

> *Every life carries gifts meant not only for personal success, but for the healing of the world.*

Human beings are not all shaped for the same work. Some are naturally drawn toward contemplation, wisdom, study, and teaching. Others carry the strength to protect, organize, lead, and stand steady during difficult seasons. Some nourish communities through practical labor, craftsmanship, caregiving, growing food, building homes, or sustaining the ordinary structures of life that others depend upon every day.

The sacred moves through many different forms of service.

And none of them are small.

Modern culture often measures worth through visibility, status, wealth, or influence. People quietly absorb the belief that only certain kinds of work matter. Public success becomes more honored than hidden faithfulness. Yet a society cannot survive through leadership alone. It is held together by countless unseen acts of care and responsibility.

A parent comforting a frightened child.

A nurse tending gently to the exhausted.

A worker showing up honestly day after day.

A teacher patiently helping another human being grow.

A person preparing meals, repairing broken things, listening deeply, cleaning spaces others overlook.

These acts sustain life more than the world often realizes.

The soul suffers when it spends too long trying to become someone it was never shaped to be. Many people exhaust themselves chasing identities built mostly from comparison and cultural pressure. They ask, “How can I become important?” instead of listening more deeply for the quieter question beneath it all:

How am I meant to love?

For vocation is not merely career.

It is participation.

A way the soul offers its particular gifts back into the world.

This does not mean every task will always feel easy or fulfilling. Even meaningful work carries difficulty, sacrifice, repetition, and seasons of weariness. But there is a difference between exhaustion that comes from sincere offering and exhaustion that comes from abandoning oneself entirely in pursuit of approval or status.

The heart knows this difference.

A healthy society recognizes the dignity woven into many forms of labor. It honors wisdom without turning wisdom into elitism. It honors leadership without allowing power to become domination. It values commerce without sacrificing compassion. It respects practical work instead of treating service as lesser.

For whenever greed overtakes vocation, something sacred begins to erode.

Leadership becomes control.

Work becomes exploitation.

People become tools instead of living souls.

And the human family grows fragmented.

Perhaps this is why humility matters so deeply. No single person carries every gift. We belong to one another in ways modern culture often forgets. The visible and invisible forms of service all matter. The sacred moves quietly through ordinary faithfulness far more often than through spectacle.

And perhaps the deeper question is not whether our work makes us impressive,

but whether it helps life become more whole.

### Breath Prayer

> Inhale: *Show me the work that is mine*
>
> Exhale: *Let me serve with humility*
