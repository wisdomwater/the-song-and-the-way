## 69 — The Kind of Happiness That Heals

> *Not everything that feels good leads to life. Not everything difficult leads to harm.*

## The Song

Krishna spoke
of happiness.

Not all happiness,
He said,
leads in the same direction.

Some forms of happiness
taste bitter
at first.

The discipline feels hard.

The slowing down
feels uncomfortable.

The healing
requires honesty.

The ego resists surrender.

The restless mind
fights silence.

At first,
it feels like medicine.

But over time,
this path becomes nectar.

Peace deepens.

Clarity awakens.

The soul remembers itself.

This is the happiness
born from goodness.

Then there is
another kind.

Sweet at first.

Immediate.

Exciting.

Pleasurable.

The rush
of indulgence.

The intoxication
of desire fulfilled.

The thrill
of escape,
consumption,
attention,
control.

For a moment,
it feels like nectar.

But eventually
it leaves bitterness behind.

Exhaustion.

Attachment.

Emptiness.

Restlessness.

The ache
for more.

This is the happiness
of passion.

Then there is
the happiness
of forgetfulness.

The comfort
of numbness.

The safety
of avoidance.

Sleep without awakening.

Distraction without meaning.

Withdrawal from life itself.

A false peace
that slowly dims
the soul.

This happiness,
Krishna said,
belongs to darkness.

And then He reminds Arjuna:

No one living
in this world
is untouched
by these forces.

Every human heart
moves among them.

### Along the Way

This teaching feels
astonishingly relevant.

Modern culture
often defines happiness
as immediate gratification.

If it feels good now,
it must be good.

If it feels difficult,
it must be avoided.

But Krishna challenges
that assumption.

Some things
that nourish the soul
initially feel uncomfortable.

Therapy can feel painful
before it becomes healing.

Honest self-examination
can sting
before it frees us.

Learning discipline,
patience,
forgiveness,
or love
often feels awkward
before it becomes natural.

Even physical healing
works this way.

The body resists change
before it grows stronger.

The soul does too.

Meanwhile,
many pleasures
that feel wonderful
in the moment
slowly hollow us out.

Constant distraction.

Compulsive consumption.

Endless stimulation.

Emotional numbing.

Living entirely
for achievement,
approval,
or escape.

At first,
these soothe discomfort.

But eventually,
they leave us fragmented.

Krishna is inviting us
to ask a deeper question
than:
“What feels good right now?”

Instead:

“What kind of life
is this creating within me?”

Does this path
make me more awake?

More loving?

More grounded?

More free?

Or does it slowly
pull me away
from myself?

The deepest joy
is rarely the loudest.

It often grows quietly.

Like roots
stretching downward
before branches
ever appear.

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“Lead me toward what is true”

Breathing out:
“Even when the path feels difficult”
