## 65 — The Freedom of Letting Go

> *Renunciation is not the rejection of life.  
> It is the release of the self-centered grasping that keeps us from love.*

## The Song

Arjuna asked:

“What is true renunciation?

And what does it mean
to let go?”

Krishna answered gently.

Some believe,
He said,
that all action itself
is flawed.

That holiness means withdrawal.

Escape.

Separation from the world.

But others understand
something deeper.

The problem
is not action itself.

The problem
is attachment.

The endless hunger
to act
for personal gain,
recognition,
control,
or reward.

True renunciation
is not inactivity.

It is freedom
from possessiveness.

It is learning
to act
without making the self
the center
of every motion.

The wise still work.

They still serve.

They still love.

They still speak,
create,
care,
build,
teach,
weep,
and carry responsibility.

But they no longer live
only for themselves.

Their lives
become offerings.

Not performances.

Not transactions.

Not attempts
to secure identity
through achievement.

Krishna described
the one who acts
without clinging
to the fruits
of action.

Who gives fully,
yet releases the need
to control outcomes.

Who serves
without constantly asking:

“What do I get in return?”

This,
He said,
is true freedom.

### Along the Way

Many people imagine
spiritual life
as escape from ordinary life.

Leaving the world.

Avoiding responsibility.

Withdrawing from relationships,
work,
difficulty,
or sorrow.

But Krishna points
toward another path.

The transformation
is not merely external.

It is interior.

A person may leave society
yet remain deeply attached
to ego,
image,
comfort,
or superiority.

And another person
may raise children,
work a difficult job,
wash dishes,
care for aging parents,
or struggle through ordinary life —
while quietly becoming free.

The difference
is not always visible.

The deeper question is:

What possesses the heart?

Attachment
is exhausting
because it constantly demands
certainty and reward.

“If I do this,
will I succeed?”

“If I love,
will I be loved back?”

“If I sacrifice,
will I finally matter?”

But love cannot flourish
where everything
is treated
like a transaction.

Krishna invites us
into another way of being:

To act sincerely.

To love deeply.

To serve faithfully.

And then,
to release the desperate need
to control the outcome.

This does not create passivity.

It creates peace.

Because the soul
slowly learns
that its worth
does not depend
on applause,
success,
or visible victory.

Even small acts
can become sacred
when offered freely.

A meal prepared with care.

Work done honestly.

Listening patiently.

Remaining kind
when bitterness would be easier.

These too
are forms
of holy surrender.

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“Teach me to act with love”

Breathing out:
“Teach me to let go”
