## 23 — The Flame in the Still Place

> *The soul grows quiet not by force,  
> but by learning where to rest.*

## The Song

Krishna now turned Arjuna
toward the practice of stillness.

The yogi, He said,
seeks a quiet place.

Not as an escape from the world,
but as a space where the scattered self
can become whole again.

Alone,
without grasping,
without possessiveness,
the seeker gathers the mind inward.

A simple seat is prepared.

Not too high.

Not too low.

Clean,
steady,
set apart.

The body becomes still.

The spine rises.

The gaze softens.

The breath settles.

The senses,
which are always running outward,
begin to return.

The mind,
so accustomed to wandering,
is invited back to one center.

Again and again,
the soul turns toward the Beloved
as its highest aim.

And in this turning,
peace begins to descend.

Not the peace of numbness.

Not the peace of avoidance.

But the deep peace
that comes when desire loosens
and the heart remembers
where it belongs.

Yet Krishna did not teach extremity.

The path of yoga
is not found in harshness toward the body.

Not in eating too much.

Not in eating too little.

Not in sleeping constantly.

Not in refusing rest.

The path opens through balance.

Through a life ordered gently.

Through food enough to sustain.

Sleep enough to restore.

Work held with measure.

Recreation kept in harmony.

For the body is not the enemy.

The body is the place
where the soul learns steadiness.

When the mind becomes quiet
and rests in the self,
the yogi is said to be established.

Free from the endless pull
of craving.

Like a lamp in a windless place,
the mind no longer flickers wildly.

Its flame becomes clear.

Steady.

Untroubled.

In that stillness,
the seeker begins to perceive
a joy beyond the reach of the senses.

A happiness not dependent
on what can be gained,
held,
tasted,
seen,
or praised.

This joy is subtle.

Deep.

Difficult to explain.

But once found,
the soul knows
there is nothing higher.

Even great sorrow
cannot fully uproot the one
who has discovered this hidden ground.

This,
Krishna said,
is yoga:

the severing of the soul’s union
with unnecessary suffering.

The patient release
of the desires born from imagination.

The gathering of the senses.

The steady turning of the mind
toward what is real.

### Along the Way

This passage gives us
a mercifully practical vision
of the spiritual life.

It does not begin
with grand achievement.

It begins with a place.

A posture.

A breath.

A willingness to become quiet.

So much of our suffering comes
from living dispersed.

Our attention is pulled outward
by every sound,
every desire,
every fear,
every imagined future.

We become strangers to ourselves.

The Gita invites us
to come home.

But this homecoming is not violent.

The mind is not conquered
by hatred of the body.

The heart is not healed
through contempt for ordinary needs.

Spiritual maturity requires rhythm.

Enough food.

Enough rest.

Enough work.

Enough silence.

A life without measure
becomes difficult to inhabit.

Too much indulgence clouds the heart.

Too much denial hardens it.

Balance becomes a form of wisdom.

A way of honoring the human frame
while gently training it
toward freedom.

The image of the lamp
is especially tender.

The goal is not to extinguish desire
by becoming lifeless.

The goal is to become sheltered enough
that the inner flame can burn clearly.

Most of us know what it feels like
to live in the wind.

One moment anxious.

The next defensive.

The next craving comfort.

The next ashamed of needing comfort.

But with practice,
a quieter place opens within.

A place where we can notice desire
without obeying it.

Notice fear
without being ruled by it.

Notice sorrow
without losing the ground beneath us.

This is not instant peace.

It is cultivated peace.

The kind that grows
through daily returning.

And perhaps the deepest promise here
is that joy is not only found outside us.

There is a joy beneath acquisition.

A joy beneath applause.

A joy beneath control.

A joy that comes
when the soul rests in what is real.

This joy does not remove every grief.

But it gives grief a different room to live in.

It teaches the heart
that even in calamity,
there is a steadiness deeper than circumstance.

A flame held in stillness.

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“Gather me into stillness”

Breathing out:
“Let my inner flame rest”
