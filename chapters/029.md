## 29 — Remember Me and Live

> *What we remember shapes what we become.  
> The heart learns its final song by practicing it now.*

## The Song

Arjuna asked for clarity.

What is the Imperishable?

What is the self?

What is karma?

What is this changing world?

What are the unseen powers?

What is sacrifice?

And how can the Holy be known
at the hour of death?

Krishna answered simply.

The Imperishable is the Supreme.

The self is the soul’s own true nature.

Karma is the movement
by which beings are born,
live,
act,
and pass again into change.

The world of matter
is always dissolving.

Forms arise.

Forms fade.

Bodies come and go.

But within all beings,
the sacred Presence remains.

And Krishna said:

I am the One
to whom every offering is ultimately made.

I dwell within the body.

I am nearer than breath.

Then He spoke
of the final crossing.

Whoever leaves the body
remembering Me
comes to My own nature.

Of this,
there is no doubt.

Whatever the heart remembers
at the end,
toward that it moves.

Whatever has shaped the mind
through long practice,
whatever has filled the inner life,
whatever has become most loved —
that rises at the threshold.

So Krishna said:

Remember Me always.

And fight.

Do the work before you.

Stand in the life you have been given.

But surrender your mind
and your understanding to Me.

Let remembrance live
not only in temples,
not only in silence,
not only in retreat,
but in the field itself.

In conflict.

In responsibility.

In grief.

In action.

In the ordinary pressure of being human.

Then your life
will move toward Me.

Krishna described the One
upon whom the heart should meditate:

Ancient,
yet ever near.

Subtler than the smallest particle,
yet holding all things together.

Radiant like the sun.

Beyond darkness.

Beyond decay.

Beyond every form
the mind can grasp.

At the final hour,
the one whose heart has practiced remembrance,
whose mind has become steady,
whose devotion has become strength,
passes toward the Divine Person.

The life that has learned to turn
does not forget where home is.

### Along the Way

This passage brings spiritual practice
into the presence of death.

Not morbidly.

Not fearfully.

But honestly.

Death reveals what has been forming in us all along.

The mind does not become clear at the end
by accident.

The heart remembers at the threshold
what it has practiced loving throughout life.

This is why remembrance matters.

Not as anxious religious performance.

Not as a desperate attempt
to secure the future.

But as the shaping of the soul
through daily attention.

We become like what we return to.

If we return constantly to fear,
fear forms us.

If we return constantly to resentment,
resentment forms us.

If we return constantly to craving,
craving forms us.

But if we return to the Eternal,
something steadier begins to grow.

The Gita does not ask Arjuna
to abandon his life
in order to remember.

It says:

Remember,
and fight.

Remember,
and act.

Remember,
and do the work before you.

This is a deeply embodied spirituality.

It does not separate devotion
from responsibility.

It teaches us to carry remembrance
into the very places
where we are most tempted to forget.

Into stress.

Into decisions.

Into family.

Into work.

Into uncertainty.

Into the ache of mortality.

The final moment is not isolated
from the rest of life.

It gathers the life we have been living.

So the invitation is gentle,
but serious:

Practice now
the remembrance you hope will hold you then.

Turn now
toward the Presence
you hope to recognize
when everything else falls away.

And there is mercy here.

The Holy One is not merely waiting
at the end of the road.

The Holy One dwells within the body,
within the breath,
within the hidden chamber of the heart.

Nearer than fear.

Nearer than death.

Nearer than our forgetting.

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“Teach my heart to remember”

Breathing out:
“Hold me in the final crossing”
