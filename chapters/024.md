## 24 - Returning to the Self

> *The mind wanders like wind over open water. Practice is the gentle art of returning.*

The mind rarely stays still for long.

It drifts toward old memories,
unfinished conversations,
imagined futures,
small fears that grow large in the dark.

One moment it reaches for comfort.
The next it rehearses shame.
Then suddenly it is building entire worlds out of things that have not even happened.

Many people become discouraged by this.

They sit quietly for a moment hoping for peace,
only to discover how restless they truly are.

The thoughts multiply.
The body fidgets.
The emotions rise unexpectedly.

And so they conclude:
I must be failing.

But the deeper wisdom traditions speak differently.

They remind us that wandering is simply part of being human.

The mind moves.
The heart trembles.
Attention drifts.

The practice is not perfection.

The practice is return.

Again and again,
return.

Not harshly.
Not with contempt toward oneself.

Gently.

Like guiding a child back toward safety after they have wandered too close to the edge of the road.

This changes the entire spirit of spiritual life.

Stillness is no longer something we conquer through force.

It becomes a relationship we slowly grow into.

Each time the mind runs toward fear,
we notice.

And return.

Each time we become lost in comparison,
resentment,
fantasy,
or anxious striving,
we notice.

And return.

At first this may seem small.

Almost insignificant.

But over time, these repeated returns begin reshaping the inner world.

A little more space appears between thought and reaction.
Between emotion and identity.

We begin realizing that not every voice inside the mind deserves obedience.

Fear speaks loudly.
So does shame.
So does craving.

But beneath them all lives a quieter awareness that does not panic so easily.

A steadier place.

The soul slowly learns how to stand there.

This inward steadiness also changes the way we encounter others.

When a person is constantly trapped inside their own mental storms, it becomes difficult to truly see anyone clearly.

Others become threats,
competitors,
obstacles,
or mirrors reflecting our own insecurity.

But as the heart quiets,
vision softens.

We begin recognizing something shared beneath outward differences.

The same longing for peace.
The same hidden wounds.
The same fragile hope carried quietly through human life.

Compassion becomes less forced then.

Less performative.

More natural.

Another person’s sorrow no longer feels entirely separate from our own humanity.
Another person’s joy no longer feels like something that diminishes us.

This does not erase wisdom or discernment.

Healthy boundaries still matter.
Truth still matters.

But the heart grows less defended.

More spacious.

There is also deep comfort in the honesty of this path.

The ancient teachers did not pretend the mind was easy to govern.

They compared it to wind —
restless,
strong,
difficult to contain.

And perhaps many people need to hear this.

Struggle does not mean failure.

Wandering does not mean the path is lost.

The work is simply to keep returning.

To breathe again.
To notice again.
To come home again.

Little by little,
the soul learns where to rest.

### Breath Prayer

> Inhale: *Gather my wandering mind*
>
> Exhale: *Bring me gently home*
