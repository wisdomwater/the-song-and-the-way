## 24 — Returning to the Self

> *The mind wanders like wind over open water.  
> Practice is the gentle art of returning.*

## The Song

Krishna continued:

Let the mind become still
little by little.

Not all at once.

Not by force.

Not through anger at its wandering.

But gradually,
with patient intelligence
and a steady heart.

Let the mind rest in the self.

Let it return
from every distraction,
every craving,
every anxious story.

Whenever it wanders —
as it surely will —
bring it back.

When it runs toward desire,
bring it back.

When it trembles with fear,
bring it back.

When it circles old wounds,
bring it back.

When it chases imagined futures,
bring it back.

The mind is restless,
flickering,
unsteady.

Yet it can be guided.

And as the passions settle,
as the inner noise becomes quiet,
a deeper joy begins to rise.

The yogi who practices in this way
touches a happiness
not born from possession,
pleasure,
or praise.

It is the happiness
of contact with the Eternal.

A quiet bliss
beneath the surface of life.

Then Krishna widened the vision.

The one joined to the Supreme
begins to see differently.

They see the Holy One
dwelling in all beings,
and all beings held within the Holy One.

Nothing is outside this presence.

No creature forgotten.

No life abandoned.

No heart unseen.

The one who sees Me everywhere,
Krishna said,
and sees everything in Me —
I am never lost to them,
and they are never lost to Me.

Wherever such a person lives,
whatever their outward circumstances,
they abide in union.

And the highest yogi,
Krishna said,
is the one who feels the joy and sorrow of others
as their own.

Not as sentiment.

Not as performance.

But as true seeing.

The boundary of the isolated self
has begun to soften.

Another’s pain is no longer distant.

Another’s happiness is no longer a threat.

Compassion becomes natural
because vision has become whole.

But Arjuna listened
and felt the difficulty.

He said:

Krishna,
this path sounds beautiful,
but I do not see how it can remain steady.

The mind is so restless.

So turbulent.

So powerful.

So stubborn.

Trying to control it
feels like trying to hold the wind.

And Krishna did not dismiss him.

He did not shame him.

He answered with tenderness:

Yes, mighty one.

The mind is restless.

It is difficult to control.

But it can be trained
through practice
and detachment.

For the one whose mind remains ungoverned,
yoga is hard to attain.

But for the one who keeps returning,
who learns the right way,
who practices with sincerity —
the path is possible.

### Along the Way

This passage is one of the most merciful
in the whole spiritual journey.

It tells the truth about the mind.

The mind wanders.

It resists stillness.

It rehearses fear.

It builds imaginary arguments.

It reaches backward into regret
and forward into anxiety.

It can be stubborn,
turbulent,
and exhausting.

And yet the answer is not despair.

The answer is return.

Again and again,
return.

This is deeply compassionate wisdom.

Many people imagine spiritual maturity
as a state where the mind never wanders,
where desire never rises,
where fear never speaks.

But the Gita gives us a gentler path.

The practice is not never being distracted.

The practice is noticing distraction
and coming home.

Little by little.

With patience.

With humility.

With courage.

This kind of practice changes the heart.

It creates space
between impulse and action.

Between thought and identity.

Between feeling and obedience.

We begin to realize
that not every thought is truth.

Not every fear is command.

Not every desire is destiny.

And as the mind becomes steadier,
our vision of others begins to change.

Inner stillness is never merely private.

It opens the eyes.

The one who is grounded in the self
begins to see the same sacred presence
shining through every life.

This does not erase difference.

It does not deny harm.

It does not make wisdom passive.

But it does deepen compassion.

The suffering of another
is no longer something to ignore.

The joy of another
is no longer something to envy.

Love becomes less selective.

The heart becomes less defended.

Arjuna’s honesty matters here.

He names the difficulty
that so many people feel.

How can I live this way
when my own mind feels like a storm?

And Krishna’s answer is not condemnation.

It is hope.

Practice.

Detachment.

Return.

The wind may be strong,
but the soul can learn
where to stand.

### Breath Prayer

Breathing in:
“Gather my wandering mind”

Breathing out:
“Bring me gently home”
