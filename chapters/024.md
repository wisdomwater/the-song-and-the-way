## 24 — Returning to the Self

> *The spiritual life is not the absence of wandering, but the gentle practice of returning.*

The mind rarely remains still for long. It drifts toward old memories, unfinished conversations, imagined futures, small fears that quietly grow larger in the dark. One moment it reaches for comfort. The next it rehearses shame. Then suddenly it is building entire worlds out of things that have not even happened.

Many people become discouraged when they first begin seeking stillness.

They sit quietly for a few moments hoping for peace, only to discover how restless they truly are. Thoughts multiply. The body fidgets. Emotions rise unexpectedly from beneath the surface. And so they conclude that they must be failing.

But the deeper wisdom traditions speak differently.

They remind us that wandering is simply part of being human.

The mind moves.

The heart trembles.

Attention drifts.

The practice is not perfection.

The practice is return.

Again and again, return.

Not harshly. Not with contempt toward oneself. Gently. Like guiding a child back toward safety after they have wandered too close to the edge of the road. This changes the entire spirit of the spiritual life. Stillness is no longer something conquered through force. It becomes a relationship we slowly learn how to inhabit.

Each time the mind runs toward fear, we notice.

And return.

Each time we become lost in comparison, resentment, fantasy, or anxious striving, we notice.

And return.

At first these small returns may seem almost insignificant. But over time they begin quietly reshaping the inner world. A little more space appears between thought and reaction. Between emotion and identity. We begin realizing that not every voice inside the mind deserves obedience.

Fear speaks loudly.

So does shame.

So does craving.

But beneath them all lives another awareness that does not panic so easily. A steadier place beneath the noise. And slowly, through practice, the soul learns how to stand there more often.

This inward steadiness changes the way we encounter others as well. When a person is trapped constantly inside their own mental storms, it becomes difficult to truly see anyone clearly. Others become threats, competitors, obstacles, or mirrors reflecting our own insecurity. But as the heart begins to quiet, vision softens too.

We begin recognizing something shared beneath outward differences. The same longing for peace. The same hidden wounds. The same fragile hope quietly carried through human life. Compassion becomes less performative then. Less forced. More natural. Another person’s sorrow no longer feels entirely separate from our own humanity. Another person’s joy no longer feels like something that diminishes us.

This does not erase discernment. Wisdom still sees clearly. Boundaries still matter. Truth still matters. But the heart grows less defended. More spacious. Less consumed with protecting its own fragile image.

There is deep comfort in the honesty of this path.

The ancient teachers never pretended the mind was easy to govern. They compared it to the wind — restless, strong, difficult to contain. And perhaps many people need to hear this with tenderness:

Struggle does not mean failure.

Wandering does not mean the path is lost.

The work is simply to keep returning.

To breathe again.

To notice again.

To come home again.

Little by little, the soul learns where to rest.

### Breath Prayer

> Inhale: Gather my wandering mind
>
> Exhale: Bring me gently home
