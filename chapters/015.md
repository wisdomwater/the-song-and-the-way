## 15 - The Fire That Cannot Be Fed

> *What we continually feed will eventually rule us.*

There are fires within the human heart that grow stronger each time they are obeyed.

The longing for more.
More pleasure.
More recognition.
More control.
More certainty.
More distraction from the ache we do not wish to face.

At first these desires often appear small.

A passing thought.
A private fantasy.
A moment of resentment.
A craving for comfort after exhaustion or pain.

But what the mind returns to repeatedly begins shaping the inner world.

Attention deepens into attachment.
Attachment hardens into hunger.

And hunger, when continually fed, rarely says:
“This is enough.”

Many people know this feeling intimately.

The impulse to check again.
To consume again.
To win again.
To be reassured again.
To soothe emptiness with another purchase, another achievement, another indulgence, another distraction.

Yet beneath the temporary relief, something remains unsatisfied.

Like trying to fill a bottomless vessel.

The ancient teachers understood this deeply.

They saw how craving clouds perception the way smoke fills a room.
Not destroying the light entirely,
but obscuring it.

A person under the rule of compulsion slowly loses clarity.

The mind begins rationalizing what the heart secretly wants.
The body grows restless.
Discernment weakens.
Even wisdom becomes distorted in service to appetite.

And still the hunger asks for more.

This is why unchecked desire eventually exhausts the soul.

Not because pleasure itself is evil,
but because the human spirit was never meant to build its entire life around consumption.

The senses are beautiful servants.
They allow us to taste bread,
hear music,
hold another person’s hand,
feel rain upon the skin,
laugh at a crowded table.

But when the senses become masters, the inner world grows unstable.

The soul becomes dragged endlessly outward,
searching for completion in things that cannot finally provide it.

Many people respond to this struggle by trying to suppress desire entirely.

They tighten inwardly.
Judge themselves harshly.
Attempt to become pure through force.

But repression alone often creates deeper shame and hidden fragmentation.

The quieter path is more honest than that.

To notice desire without immediately surrendering to it.
To sit beside the craving long enough to understand what deeper hunger lives beneath it.

For many compulsions are not truly about pleasure at all.

They are attempts to soothe loneliness.
Fear.
Grief.
Weariness.
The ache of feeling unloved or disconnected from oneself.

And so healing begins not through violence against the self,
but through awareness.

A slow returning to presence.

The discovery that there is something deeper within us than every passing impulse.

A steadier place.
A witnessing place.
A place capable of choosing rather than merely reacting.

This deeper center does not erase desire.

It gently reorders it.

The soul begins to hunger less for endless stimulation
and more for what is real:
rest,
truth,
connection,
beauty,
silence,
love.

And over time, the fires that once ruled the heart begin to lose their power.

Not because they were hated,
but because something deeper was finally fed.
